EMPLOYMENT & LABOUR LAW — INDIA (Legal Knowledge Base for RAG)
1. CONSTITUTIONAL FRAMEWORK
Articles 14, 15, 16 — Equality and non-discrimination
The Constitution prohibits discrimination in employment on grounds of religion, race, caste, sex, or place of birth . Article 16 guarantees equality of opportunity in public employment. The Labour Codes now explicitly prohibit gender-based discrimination, including against transgender persons, in recruitment, wages, or work conditions .

Article 21 — Right to life and personal liberty
In the employment context, Article 21 encompasses the right to a safe working environment, fair treatment, and protection from sexual harassment. The Occupational Safety, Health and Working Conditions Code, 2020 operationalises this through mandatory safety protocols and health check-ups .

Directive Principles of State Policy
These guide the State in ensuring fair wages, humane working conditions, and social security for all workers .

2. THE FOUR LABOUR CODES — 2025 IMPLEMENTATION
Historic Reform — Effective from 21 November 2025
In a landmark decision, the Government of India implemented four Labour Codes, rationalising 29 existing central labour laws into a unified framework . This marks a decisive shift away from fragmented, colonial-era legislation .

2.1 Code on Wages, 2019
Key Features:

Universal Minimum Wages: Every worker, irrespective of industry or skill category, is entitled to minimum wages — a statutory right for all employees .

National Floor Wage: The Central Government will fix a floor wage; States cannot set minimum wages below this level .

Standard Definition of Wages: A single unified definition (basic + dearness allowance + retaining allowance) reduces disputes and brings uniformity in calculations for PF, ESIC, gratuity, and bonus .

Equal Remuneration: Equal pay for men and women for similar work, with pay differences justified only by skill, effort, experience, or responsibility .

Timely Wage Payment: Mandatory timelines for monthly, weekly, and daily wage payments; no unauthorised deductions .

Overtime: Work beyond prescribed hours requires consent and payment of at least double the normal wage rate .

2.2 Industrial Relations Code, 2020
Key Features:

Fixed-Term Employment (FTE): Formally recognised; FTEs receive all benefits equal to permanent workers, including gratuity after just one year (instead of five) .

Trade Union Recognition: A uniform law now applies — if only one union exists, it is recognised as the sole negotiating union; if multiple unions exist, the union with 51% or more worker membership is recognised .

Model Standing Orders: In the service sector, work-from-home is permitted based on mutual agreement .

Threshold Changes: Prior approval requirement for retrenchment and closure applies to establishments with 300 or more workers (increased from 100) .

Reskilling Fund: Employers must contribute 15 days' wages per retrenched worker for retraining .

Two-Member Industrial Tribunals: Judicial + administrative members introduced for quicker dispute resolution .

2.3 Code on Social Security, 2020
Key Features:

Universal Social Security: Coverage extended to organised, unorganised, gig, and platform workers .

Gig and Platform Workers: Defined for the first time; aggregators must contribute 1–2% of annual turnover (capped at 5% of payments to such workers) .

ESIC Expansion: Pan-India coverage; voluntary for establishments with fewer than 10 employees; mandatory for establishments with even one employee engaged in hazardous processes .

Gratuity: Uniform one-year eligibility threshold for all establishments governed by Shops and Establishments Acts and factories .

Commute-related Accidents: Now treated as employment-related, making employees eligible for compensation .

Aadhaar-linked Universal Account Number: Provides portability of welfare benefits across states, regardless of migration .

2.4 Occupational Safety, Health and Working Conditions Code, 2020 (OSHW)
Key Features:

Universal Application: Safety, health, and welfare standards apply to establishments with 10 or more workers, and even establishments with one employee engaged in hazardous occupations .

Mandatory Appointment Letters: Every worker must receive a formal appointment letter specifying terms of employment — a major formalisation reform .

Free Annual Health Check-ups: Mandatory for all workers above the age of 40 years .

Women's Employment: Women permitted to work at night and in all types of work, including underground mining and heavy machinery, subject to their consent and mandatory safety measures .

Single Registration and Licence: Consolidates multiple registrations into one; "Inspector-cum-Facilitator" system replaces traditional inspectors .

Expanded Migrant Worker Definition: Includes contract-based and self-migrated workers, with provisions for annual lump-sum travel allowance and PDS portability .

National OSH Advisory Board: Sets mandatory national safety standards .

3. KEY DEFINITIONS & CATEGORISATIONS
Worker v. Non-Worker Employees
Workers: Employees engaged in manual, operational, technical, clerical, or similar functions — excludes those in managerial, administrative, or higher-level supervisory roles .

Non-Worker Employees: Governed mainly by State Shops and Establishments Acts and their employment contracts .

Employee (broad definition): Under the Codes, includes everyone hired for wages, whether workers, supervisors, or managerial staff .

Fixed-Term Employee (FTE)
A worker hired for a fixed duration, now formally recognised, receiving all benefits equal to permanent workers, including gratuity after one year .

Gig Worker / Platform Worker
Newly defined categories under the Social Security Code, 2020, covering non-traditional work arrangements; aggregators must contribute to their social security .

Inter-State Migrant Worker
Under the OSHW Code, includes workers employed by a contractor and self-migrated workers, ensuring portability of benefits .

4. EMPLOYMENT TERMS & CONDITIONS
Written Contracts
Previously not mandated under central law. Now mandatory — the OSHW Code requires employers to give every employee a written appointment letter specifying role, wages, working hours, leave, and notice period .

Working Hours & Overtime
Maximum: 9 hours per day, 48 hours per week .

Overtime: Work beyond prescribed hours must be consensual, paid at double the normal wage rate .

Leaves
Paid annual leave, sick leave, casual leave, and national holidays remain governed by applicable statutes.

Under the OSHW Code, workers can avail annual leaves after 180 days of work in a year .

Maternity Leave
26 weeks of paid maternity leave (8 weeks pre-delivery) for women with fewer than two children; 12 weeks for women with two or more surviving children .

Right to return to same or equivalent position with no reduction in pay, seniority, or service benefits .

Crèche facility required in establishments with 50+ employees; four visits per day allowed for nursing .

Paternity Leave
No statutory right — depends entirely on employer policy .

5. DISCRIMINATION & PROTECTIONS
Anti-Discrimination
Sex-based discrimination in recruitment, wages, and service conditions is prohibited .

Equal pay for equal work — wage differences only justified by skill, effort, or responsibility .

No discrimination against transgender persons .

Sexual Harassment (POSH Act, 2013)
Internal Committee required in every workplace .

Written POSH policy, awareness notices, and regular employee sensitisation programmes are mandatory .

Annual POSH report filing required .

Labour Codes do not replace POSH obligations — they continue in full force .

Whistleblowing
No general whistleblower protection law for private employees.

Only listed companies and certain regulated sectors have mandatory vigil mechanisms under the Companies Act and SEBI rules .

6. TERMINATION & DISPUTE RESOLUTION
Retrenchment (Workers)
Notice/compensation: One-month notice or payment in lieu; retrenchment compensation for workers with one year of continuous service.

Prior approval: Required for establishments with 300 or more workers (Code on IR) .

Reskilling Fund: Employer must contribute 15 days' wages per retrenched worker .

Termination (Non-Workers)
Governed by employment contract and State Shops and Establishments Acts .

Collective Bargaining & Trade Unions
Negotiating union recognition based on 51% membership threshold .

If no union meets threshold, a Negotiating Council is formed .

Industrial Action
Workers must give 14 days' prior notice before a strike .

Strike not a fundamental right — legally regulated .

Failure to follow procedure renders strike illegal .

Dispute Resolution
Two-Member Industrial Tribunals (judicial + administrative) for quicker resolution .

Approach tribunals directly after conciliation .

Electronic records, registration, and communication permitted under the Codes .

Quashing of FIRs on Settlement
The Delhi High Court in November 2025 reiterated that FIRs under BNS for cheating (including job fraud) may be quashed if:

The dispute is personal in nature

Settlement is voluntary

No broader public interest is affected

No other victims are identified

Non-compoundable offences under BNS can be quashed under Section 528 BNSS (inherent powers) .

7. CYBERCRIME OVERLAP — EMPLOYMENT FRAUD
BNS Provisions in Cyber-Fraud Cases
Section 318(4) BNS — Cheating by personation (e.g., impersonating recruitment executives) .
Section 319(2) BNS — Cheating and dishonestly inducing delivery of property .
Section 61 BNS — Organised crime (includes cybercrime) .

Work-from-Home Scams
Victims lured via WhatsApp/Telegram with promises of high returns, often using "work-from-home" offers .

Police have invoked Section 318(4) and Section 319(2) BNS in such cases .

Job Fraud Syndicates
Accused lure victims with job promises, collect banking credentials, misuse accounts, and launder money online .

Offences under BNS and IT Act, 2000 .

Settlement-Based Quashing in Job Fraud Cases
If settlement is reached and complainant is compensated, Courts may quash FIR under Section 528 BNSS .

Delhi High Court required ₹5,000 deposit with Advocates' Welfare Trust as condition .

8. FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS
Q1: What changed with the Labour Codes from 21 November 2025?
A: The four Labour Codes replaced 29 central labour laws, introducing universal minimum wages, mandatory appointment letters, expanded social security, and national standards for safety and working conditions .

Q2: Is a written employment contract now required?
A: Yes. Under the Occupational Safety, Health and Working Conditions Code, 2020, every employer must issue a written appointment letter to every worker .

Q3: What are my overtime rights?
A: Overtime for work beyond prescribed hours (9 hours/day, 48 hours/week) requires consent and must be paid at double the normal wage rate .

Q4: Do I get maternity leave if I'm a gig worker?
A: Under the Social Security Code, 2020, gig and platform workers are covered under the social security net, and schemes are being formulated for such workers .

Q5: What rights do fixed-term employees have?
A: Fixed-term employees get all benefits equal to permanent workers, including leave, medical benefits, and social security. Gratuity eligibility is after one year instead of five .

Q6: Can women work night shifts now?
A: Yes. Women are permitted to work in all types of work, including night shifts, subject to their consent and mandatory safety measures .

Q7: What if I'm a victim of a work-from-home scam?
A: Report the fraud. Offences are registered under BNS Sections 318(4) and 319(2) — cheating by personation and dishonest inducement .

Q8: Can an FIR for a cyber-fraud job scam be quashed if I settle?
A: Yes, if the offence is personal in nature, the settlement is voluntary, no larger public interest is impacted, and the State has no objection. Courts consider such quashing under Section 528 BNSS .
