# Police Act 1861 — Foundational Police Framework

## Historical Background

The Police Act, 1861 was enacted on 22 March 1861 after the Revolt of 1857.

The objective was:

- Reorganisation of police forces
- Better crime prevention
- Establishment of systematic policing structure


The Act created the foundation of modern Indian policing.


# Important Sections


## Section 2 — Constitution of Police Force

Section 2 states that the entire police establishment under a State Government forms one police force.

Features:

- Police force is formally enrolled
- Strength decided by State Government
- Police operates under state authority


## Section 3 — Superintendence of Police

Section 3 provides that superintendence of police belongs to the State Government.

The State controls:

- Police administration
- Organisation
- General supervision


## Section 4 — Inspector General of Police

Section 4 establishes administrative control through:

- Inspector General of Police
- Deputy Inspector Generals
- Assistant Inspector Generals

District police administration is handled by:

- District Superintendent of Police


## Section 7 — Appointment and Discipline

Section 7 provides disciplinary powers.

Authorities may:

- Appoint officers
- Dismiss officers
- Suspend officers
- Take disciplinary action

Subject to:

- Article 311 Constitution
- State rules


## Section 8 — Inferior Police Officers

The Act describes subordinate ranks as inferior officers.

This reflects the colonial hierarchical structure of policing.


# Criticism of Police Act 1861

Major criticisms:

- Colonial mindset
- Excessive control-oriented approach
- Lack of accountability
- Militaristic structure


The Indian Police Commission (1902) criticised police organisation for:

- Poor training
- Inefficient administration
- Corruption
- Lack of public trust


# Police Reform Context

The Police Act 1861 remains the foundation for many state police laws.

Major concerns:

- Need for democratic policing
- Citizen-friendly policing
- Greater accountability
