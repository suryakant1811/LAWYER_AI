PROPERTY LAW — INDIA (Legal Knowledge Base for RAG)
1. CONSTITUTIONAL FRAMEWORK
Article 300A — Right to Property
The Constitution guarantees that no person shall be deprived of property except by authority of law. Property rights are no longer a fundamental right but a constitutional right under Article 300A, requiring any deprivation to be backed by a valid legal framework .

Article 39(b) — Material Resources of the Community
The Directive Principle under Article 39(b) places a duty on the State to ensure that "the ownership and control of the material resources of the community are so distributed as best to subserve the common good." This provision has historically been invoked by State governments to justify acquisition of private property for public welfare purposes .

Landmark Supreme Court Ruling — Property Owners Association v. State of Maharashtra (2024)
In a landmark nine-judge bench judgment delivered on 5 November 2024, the Supreme Court held that privately owned properties of individuals cannot be treated as "material resources of the community" under Article 39(b) .

Legal Issue: Whether private properties fall within the ambit of Article 39(b) for State acquisition and distribution for public good. The Maharashtra government had amended the Maharashtra Housing and Area Development Act, 1976 (MHADA) to empower the Mumbai Building Repair and Reconstruction Board to acquire "cessed" properties for restoration with consent of 70% of residents .

Majority Opinion: The nine-judge bench overruled earlier judgments that had taken a contrary view (including State of Karnataka v. Ranganatha Reddy and Sanjeev Coke v. Bharat Coking Coal), holding that private property cannot be indiscriminately made material resources of the community. Government actions acquiring private property under the shelter of Article 31(c) cannot rely on Article 39(2); such actions can be challenged as violative of Articles 14, 19, and 21 .

Significance: This judgment protects individual property rights against excessive State intervention under the guise of public welfare, while reaffirming the State's authority to enact welfare legislation subject to constitutional safeguards .

2. TRANSFER OF PROPERTY ACT, 1882
The Transfer of Property Act, 1882 (ToPA) is the foundational statute governing the transfer of immovable property in India. It is divided into key chapters covering sale, mortgage, lease, exchange, and gift .

2.1 Sale of Immovable Property — Section 54
Definition: Sale is a transfer of ownership in exchange for a price paid or promised, or part-paid and part-promised .

Registration Requirement: A sale of tangible immovable property of a value of ₹100 or more can be made only by a registered instrument . Under the draft Registration Bill, 2025, the ₹100 threshold is proposed to be removed, making registration mandatory for all sales of immovable property .

Process: A contract for sale does not itself create any interest in the property — it merely creates an obligation to transfer. The actual transfer of ownership occurs upon registration of the sale deed .

Rights and Liabilities of Buyer and Seller: Key rights include the buyer's right to possession upon completion and the seller's right to receive the sale price. Doctrine of marshalling applies — where a subsequent purchaser can require the seller to exhaust other properties first before claiming from the property sold .

2.2 Mortgage — Sections 58 to 101
Definition: A mortgage is the transfer of an interest in specific immovable property for the purpose of securing the payment of money advanced or to be advanced by way of loan, an existing or future debt, or the performance of an engagement which may give rise to a pecuniary liability .

Types of Mortgage:

Simple Mortgage — Section 58(b): Mortgagor binds himself personally to pay; the mortgagee has no power of sale but may sue for foreclosure .

Mortgage by Conditional Sale — Section 58(c): Property is sold to mortgagee with condition that sale becomes void on repayment .

Usufructuary Mortgage — Section 58(d): Mortgagee is given possession of the property to receive rents/profits in lieu of interest .

English Mortgage — Section 58(e): Mortgagor binds himself to repay on a certain date and transfers absolute ownership subject to reconveyance on repayment .

Equitable Mortgage / Mortgage by Deposit of Title Deeds — Section 58(f): A mortgage created in notified towns by depositing title deeds with the creditor. This is the most common form of mortgage in banking and finance .

Registration of Equitable Mortgages: Under the proposed Registration Bill, 2025, documents setting out terms and conditions for an equitable mortgage must be registered, except where filed with the registering officer under Section 14(3) . Lenders must file copies of title deeds with the registrar, notifying the creation of such mortgage. Only equitable mortgages notified to the registrar will take effect against subsequently executed mortgage deeds .

Rights of Mortgagor and Mortgagee:

Mortgagor's right to redeem — the right to get back the property on repayment (Section 60) .

Mortgagee's right to foreclosure or sale (Section 67) .

Doctrine of marshalling: The mortgagor's remedy to compel the mortgagee to satisfy the debt from particular securities .

Doctrine of contribution: Where property is subject to multiple mortgages, contribution ensures equitable sharing among mortgagees .

Doctrine of subrogation: The right of a person who pays off a debt to step into the creditor's shoes .

2.3 Lease — Sections 105 to 116
Definition: A lease is a transfer of a right to enjoy immovable property made for a certain time, express or implied, or in perpetuity, in consideration of a price paid or promised, or of money, a share of crops, service or any other thing of value to be rendered periodically or on specified occasions .

Essentials:

The lessor (landlord) transfers the right to enjoy the property.

The lessee (tenant) obtains the right to possession and enjoyment.

Consideration (premium or rent) is present.

The lease is for a determinate period .

Difference Between Lease and License: A lease creates an interest in the property itself, while a license merely grants permission to do something on the property without creating any interest. The Supreme Court in Associated Hotels of India Ltd. v. R.N. Kapoor (1959) held that the intention of the parties determines whether a transaction is a lease or a license .

Registration: Leases from year to year, or for any term exceeding one year, or reserving a yearly rent, are compulsorily registrable under Section 17(1)(d) of the Registration Act. Under the Registration Bill, 2025, the definition of "lease" is significantly expanded to include any instrument by which tolls are let out, any writing on an application for a lease signifying grant, and mining leases for minor minerals .

2.4 Gift — Sections 122 to 129
Definition: A gift is the transfer of certain existing movable or immovable property made voluntarily and without consideration, by one person called the donor, to another called the donee, accepted by or on behalf of the donee .

Essentials:

Transfer must be voluntary and without consideration.

The property must be existing and transferable.

The donor must be competent to contract.

The gift must be accepted by the donee .

Onerous Gift: A gift with burdens attached — the donee must accept the gift with its burdens and cannot claim the beneficial part without accepting the burdens .

Universal Donee: The donee becomes liable for all the debts and liabilities of the donor to the extent of the property received .

Donation Mortis Causa: A gift made in contemplation of death, which takes effect only on the donor's death and is revocable before death .

2.5 Exchange — Sections 118 to 121
Definition: When two persons mutually transfer the ownership of one thing for the ownership of another, neither thing nor both being money only, the transaction is called an exchange .

Distinction from Sale: In a sale, the consideration is money; in exchange, the consideration is another property. Partition is not an exchange but a division of common property .

2.6 Charges — Sections 100 to 101
A charge is a security for the payment of money where no transfer of interest in the property takes place, but the property is made liable for the payment of the debt. It is distinguished from a mortgage where there is a transfer of an interest in the property .

3. REGISTRATION OF DOCUMENTS
3.1 Registration Act, 1908 — Current Framework
The Registration Act, 1908, a 117-year-old colonial-era statute, governs the registration of documents relating to immovable property. It provides a record of transactions, ensures legal validity, and prevents fraud .

Compulsorily Registrable Documents (Section 17):

Instruments of gift of immovable property.

Non-testamentary instruments creating, declaring, assigning, limiting, or extinguishing any right, title, or interest in immovable property valued at ₹100 or more.

Leases of immovable property from year to year, or for any term exceeding one year, or reserving yearly rent.

Agreements to sell accompanied by possession (under Section 53A of ToPA) .

Exemption: Agreements for sale that do not confer possession are not compulsorily registrable, unless a particular State has made an amendment to the contrary (e.g., Gujarat, Kerala, Madhya Pradesh, Uttar Pradesh, Tamil Nadu) .

3.2 Registration Bill, 2025 — Proposed Overhaul
The Government of India introduced the draft Registration Bill, 2025 on 27 May 2025, seeking to replace the colonial-era Registration Act, 1908, with a modern, technology-driven, paperless, and citizen-centric system .

Key Proposed Changes:

(1) Compulsory Registration Expanded — Section 12 :

Agreements for sale of immovable property (including developer's/promoter's agreements).

Powers of Attorney (PoA) authorising transfer of immovable property, with or without consideration.

Sale certificates issued by competent authorities.

Equitable mortgage arrangements (documents setting out terms, except where filed under Section 14(3)).

Instruments related to corporate restructuring (amalgamation, merger, demerger) where immovable property is transferred.

Court decrees or awards that create, limit, or extinguish any right, title, or interest in immovable property.

Adoption instruments for daughters (extended from sons).

(2) Equitable Mortgage Notification — Section 14(3) :

All banks, financial institutions, and other creditors in whose favour an equitable mortgage is created must file copies of title deeds with the registering officer within whose jurisdiction the mortgaged property is situated.

Only equitable mortgages notified to the registrar will take effect against subsequently executed mortgage deeds.

(3) Online Registration — Section 32 :

End-to-end digital registration, electronic presentation and admission of documents, electronic issuance of registration certificates, and digital maintenance of records.

Aadhaar-based authentication with informed consent; alternative verification for those without Aadhaar.

Electronic integration with other record-keeping systems.

(4) Standardised Templates — Section 33 :

Government may notify standardised templates for certain documents. Registration will not be refused if standard templates are not used.

(5) Refusal Grounds — Section 58 :

Clear, objective grounds for refusal: lack of proper translation, unattested interlineations/alterations, insufficient property description, unsatisfactory identity of executors, late presentation, documents related to Government-owned property.

(6) Cancellation of Registration — Section 64 :

Specific grounds for cancellation by an adjudicating authority (not below the rank of IGR).

Notice to all parties, show cause notice, opportunity to be heard, and recorded reasons.

Appeal to appellate authority within 30 days.

(7) Appeals Mechanism — Sections 60, 61, 63 :

Structured hierarchical appeal within registration authorities before approaching judiciary.

Reduces delays and litigation in civil courts.

(8) Amendments to Transfer of Property Act — Section 86 :

Removes the ₹100 threshold for registration of sale deeds, making registration mandatory for all sales of immovable property.

Removes the ₹100 threshold for registration of mortgages, making registration mandatory for all mortgages (other than deposit of title deeds).

(9) Fee Rationalisation — Section 70 :

Fees to have reasonable relationship with cost of providing services.

Periodic review and rationalisation.

For multi-document transactions, fees payable only for the principal document; other documents require only nominal fees.

4. REAL ESTATE (REGULATION AND DEVELOPMENT) ACT, 2016 (RERA)
4.1 Overview and Purpose
RERA was enacted by Parliament to regulate the contractual relationship between homebuyers and promoters/promoters, deriving powers from the Concurrent List of the Seventh Schedule of the Constitution . The objective is to enhance accountability and transparency in the real estate sector, facilitate fast-track grievance redressal, prevent fund diversion, and ensure timely delivery with quality construction .

4.2 Implementation Status
As of 1 September 2025:

All States/UTs except Nagaland have notified Rules and established Real Estate Regulatory Authorities .

More than 1.51 lakh projects and 1.06 lakh agents have been registered .

More than 1.47 lakh complaints disposed by Regulatory Authorities across the country .

Nagaland has not implemented RERA due to concerns about potential implications on constitutional provisions under Article 371-A. However, the State has formed an inter-departmental committee to examine applicability with appropriate amendments .

4.3 Key Features
Section 3 — Mandatory Registration :
No promoter shall advertise, market, book, sell, or offer for sale any plot, apartment, or building in any real estate project without registering the project with RERA. Projects ongoing on the date of commencement of RERA and for which completion certificate has not been issued must register within three months .

Section 13 — Escrow Account :
Promoters must deposit 70% of the project cost in a separate escrow account to be used solely for construction and land costs. Withdrawals without proper permissions are prohibited.

RERA Agreements for Sale:
For "real estate projects" as defined under RERA, promoters are required to execute and register an agreement for sale with the allottee prior to accepting any amount exceeding 10% of the total cost .

Section 59 — Penalty for Non-Registration :
Failure to comply with RERA orders/directions for non-registration attracts punishment up to 3 years imprisonment or fine up to 10% of the estimated cost of the real estate project, or both.

4.4 Ongoing Challenges and Issues
Misleading Advertisements: Unregistered project advertisements deceive homebuyers and undermine investor confidence. MoHUA has requested States/RERA Authorities to take appropriate action .

Stalled Pre-RERA Projects: The problem of stalled legacy housing projects remains persistent. The Uttar Pradesh government formulated a policy package on the lines of the Amitabh Kant Committee Report, reviving stalled projects in Noida and Greater Noida .

Non-Implementation of RERA Orders: Builders frequently fail to comply with RERA orders and directions, adversely impacting homebuyer interests. MoHUA is addressing this issue at the time of amendment of the Act .

Fund Diversion: Builders collecting money outside the designated escrow account remains a critical issue .

Recovery Certificates: While RERA issues recovery certificates, their non-implementation by revenue authorities is a significant problem. Gujarat and Uttar Pradesh RERAs are actively following up with Revenue Authorities for effective enforcement .

Best Practices :

Gujarat RERA: Fund withdrawal linked to project data verification.

UPRERA: Media team to curb misleading advertisements.

MahaRERA: Collaboration with Advertising Standards Council (ASCI) to track unregistered project advertisements.

TNRERA: Consolidated guidelines on advertising.

4.5 RERA and Real Estate Sector
RERA plays a key role in:

Regulating and promoting the real estate sector .

Enhancing accountability and transparency .

Facilitating fast-track grievance redressal .

Preventing fund diversion .

Ensuring timely delivery and quality construction .

5. DIFFERENTIATING GIFT, SETTLEMENT, AND WILL
Supreme Court Clarification (May 2025)
The Supreme Court provided clear guidelines distinguishing between 'Gift', 'Settlement', and 'Will' in property transfers, emphasizing that the substance and intent behind a document take precedence over its title or form .

Gift: A gratuitous grant by the owner to another person, voluntary and unconditional, without any expectation of return. The transfer takes effect immediately upon execution. Nomenclature is not conclusive .

Settlement: Although similarly based on love and affection, it is generally made in favour of a family member and involves mutual affection as a form of consideration. A settlement deed involves an immediate transfer of ownership, even if the donor retains certain rights or interests during their lifetime. Once executed out of love and affection, it cannot be unilaterally revoked by the donor .

Will: A declaration of the testator's intention for disposition of property in a particular manner. A will comes into force only after the testator's death. A will may include elements of gifting, but the timing of the transfer is the primary distinction .

Composite Documents: A single document can possess characteristics of both a settlement and a will. In such cases, the document must be registered and interpreted using the doctrine of severability, treating the respective rights as distinct yet coexisting dispositions .

Key Principle: "Postponement of delivery by creation of life interest is not an anathema to absolute conveyance in praesenti" — reserving a life interest does not automatically classify a document as a will if ownership rights have already been transferred .

Practical Implication: A settlement deed executed out of love and affection within a family is irrevocable, safeguarding the donee's interests and ensuring stability in property ownership .

6. GLOSSARY / KEY DEFINITIONS
Sale (Section 54 ToPA): Transfer of ownership in exchange for a price paid or promised. Under the Registration Bill, 2025, all sales require registration .

Mortgage (Section 58 ToPA): Transfer of interest in immovable property to secure a debt. Equitable mortgage is created by deposit of title deeds .

Lease (Section 105 ToPA): Transfer of the right to enjoy immovable property for a determinate period in consideration of rent or other value .

Gift (Section 122 ToPA): Voluntary transfer of property without consideration, taking immediate effect .

Settlement: Transfer of property out of love and affection within a family, involving immediate transfer of ownership and irrevocability .

Will: Testamentary declaration effective only after death, revocable during lifetime .

Equitable Mortgage (Section 58(f) ToPA): Mortgage by deposit of title deeds in notified towns; requires notification under Registration Bill, 2025 .

Material Resources of the Community (Article 39(b)): Under the 2024 Supreme Court ruling, does not include privately owned property of individuals .

Right to Property (Article 300A): Constitutional right against arbitrary deprivation of property; not a fundamental right but enforceable .

7. FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS
Q1: Can the government acquire my private property under Article 39(b)?
A: No. The Supreme Court in Property Owners Association v. State of Maharashtra (2024) held that privately owned properties of individuals do not fall under "material resources of the community" under Article 39(b) .

Q2: Is registration of an agreement for sale mandatory?
A: Under the current Registration Act, it is mandatory only if accompanied by possession (Section 53A ToPA). Under the proposed Registration Bill, 2025, all agreements for sale will be compulsorily registrable .

Q3: What is an equitable mortgage and how is it affected by the new Registration Bill?
A: An equitable mortgage is created by depositing title deeds with a creditor. Under the Registration Bill, 2025, the document setting out terms must be registered, and lenders must notify the registrar of the creation of such mortgage .

Q4: Can I register a property online?
A: The Registration Bill, 2025 proposes comprehensive online registration, electronic submission and admission of documents, electronic issuance of registration certificates, and digital maintenance of records. Aadhaar-based authentication will be an option with alternative mechanisms for those without Aadhaar .

Q5: What is the difference between a gift and a settlement?
A: A gift is a voluntary, unconditional transfer without expectation of return. A settlement is made out of love and affection within a family, involves mutual affection as a form of consideration, involves an immediate transfer of ownership, and is irrevocable .

Q6: Can a settlement deed be revoked?
A: No. Once a settlement deed is executed out of love and affection, it cannot be unilaterally revoked by the donor. This includes cases where the donor retains a life interest .

Q7: Is RERA applicable to all real estate projects?
A: RERA applies to all projects in planning areas; promoters must register projects before advertising or selling. Pre-RERA projects without completion certificates also must register. Exceptions: Nagaland has not implemented due to constitutional concerns under Article 371-A .

Q8: What happens if a builder does not comply with RERA orders?
A: Section 59 RERA provides for punishment up to 3 years imprisonment or fine up to 10% of the project cost, or both. However, non-implementation of orders remains a key challenge, and the Ministry is addressing it through amendments .

Q9: Are pre-RERA projects covered by RERA?
A: Yes. Promoters of ongoing projects for which completion certificates have not been issued must register with RERA within three months of the Act's commencement .

Q10: Is a power of attorney for property transfer required to be registered?
A: Under the Registration Bill, 2025, any power of attorney authorising transfer of immovable property (with or without consideration) will be compulsorily registrable, making this requirement uniform across all States .
