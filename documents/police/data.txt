POLICE & CRIMINAL LAW — INDIA (Legal Knowledge Base for RAG)
1. CONSTITUTIONAL FRAMEWORK
Article 21 — Right to Life and Personal Liberty
The procedural safeguards governing police powers of arrest, detention, and investigation are rooted in Article 21. The Supreme Court has repeatedly held that arbitrary arrest and detention violate this fundamental right. Preventive action and detention have been held constitutional but subject to judicial checks and balances to prevent abuse .

Article 22 — Protection Against Arrest and Detention
Provides constitutional safeguards:

Right to be informed of grounds of arrest (Article 22(1))

Right to consult and be defended by a legal practitioner

Mandatory production before Magistrate within 24 hours of arrest (Article 22(2))

Exceptions for preventive detention laws (Article 22(4)(b))

Article 311 — Service Protection for Police Personnel
Governs dismissal, removal, or reduction in rank of police officers as "members of a civil service" — applicable to recruitment and disciplinary proceedings under the Police Act, 1861 .

2. POLICE ACT, 1861 — FOUNDATIONAL FRAMEWORK
Historical Background
The Police Act, 1861 (Act No. 5 of 1861) was enacted on 22 March 1861 following the 1857 Rebellion, with the objective of "reorganizing the police and making it a more efficient instrument for the prevention and detection of crime" . It established the foundational structure of the Indian Police that largely continues today .

Key Provisions
Section 2 — Constitution of the Force
The entire police establishment under a State Government is deemed one police force, formally enrolled, with numbers and constitution determined by the State Government .

Section 3 — Superintendence in the State Government
The superintendence of police throughout a general police district vests in and is exercised by the State Government. No person, officer, or Court shall be empowered to supersede or control any police functionary .

Section 4 — Inspector-General of Police
The administration of police throughout a general police district is vested in the Inspector-General of Police, with Deputy and Assistant Inspectors-General as the State Government deems fit. District-level administration is vested in the District Superintendent under the general control and direction of the Magistrate of the district .

Section 7 — Appointment, Dismissal, etc.
Subject to Article 311 of the Constitution and State rules, the Inspector-General, Deputy Inspectors-General, and District Superintendents have the power to appoint, dismiss, and discipline inferior officers .

Section 8 — Police Officers as "Inferior Officers"
The Act uses the term "inferior officers" for lower ranks, reflecting the colonial hierarchical structure where senior positions were held by British officers and subordinate ranks by Indians .

Legacy and Critiques
The 1861 Act established a militaristic and authoritarian pattern with tremendous emphasis on discipline bordering on regimentation. The Indian Police Commission (1902) itself concluded: "The police force is far from efficient; it is defective in training and organisation; it is inadequately supervised; it is generally regarded as corrupt and oppressive; and it has utterly failed to secure the confidence and cordial cooperation of the people" . Despite independence, the Police Act of 1861 has not been centrally replaced, and most state enactments are patterned on the same colonial model .

3. NEW CRIMINAL LAWS — BNSS, 2023
Overview of the Reform
Effective from 1 July 2024, India underwent a major criminal law overhaul:

BNS, 2023 — replaced IPC, 1860 (primary penal law)

BNSS, 2023 — replaced CrPC, 1973 (procedural law)

BSA, 2023 — replaced Indian Evidence Act, 1872 (evidentiary law) 

The stated aim was to "decolonise" criminal laws. However, critics note the new laws retain approximately 80 percent of text from existing legislations while expanding police powers .

Police Powers Enhancement Under BNSS
Increased Police Custody Period
The maximum period for police custody (with judicial approval) has been increased from 15 days under CrPC to 60 or 90 days under BNSS, depending on the nature of the offence . This expansion of police powers has been a subject of concern among legal commentators .

Multiple Custody Periods
BNSS permits multiple shorter periods of detention in police custody, which may be requested even beyond the first 15 days of arrest (at any time in the first 40 or 60 days) — significantly increasing the risk of police excesses .

Preventive Action — Section 172 BNSS
A person failing to comply with lawful police directions may be detained or removed. In petty cases, release must be within 24 hours, but for non-petty cases, the period may be extended. This provision does not constitute "arrest" — raising concerns about whether judicial safeguards apply .

Handcuffing — Statutory Validation
BNSS has introduced statutory provisions permitting handcuffing, despite existing judicial precedent declaring it unconstitutional in certain circumstances .

4. ACCOUNTABILITY AND TRANSPARENCY — NEW PROVISIONS
Enhanced Police Accountability Mechanisms
(1) Information Display — Section 37(b) BNSS
Each district and police station must have a designated police officer (not below ASI rank) responsible for maintaining and displaying to the public information about persons arrested, etc. .

(2) Information on Arrest Outside District — Section 82(2) BNSS
When a person is arrested under a warrant executed outside the district, the arresting officer must forthwith give information regarding such arrest and place of detention to:

The designated police officer in their district

The police officer in the other district where the arrested person normally resides 

(3) Audio-Video Recording of Search and Seizure — Section 105 BNSS
Search and seizure must be audio-video recorded, and the recording sent to the District Magistrate or Judicial Magistrate without delay. Section 185 further requires such recordings to be sent within 48 hours to the Magistrate empowered to take cognizance of the offence .

(4) Crime Scene Videography — Section 176(3) BNSS
Video-recording of crime scenes is mandated in cases of offences punishable with seven years or more imprisonment .

(5) Fortnightly Diary Report — Section 176(2) BNSS
Police officers must forward the daily diary report fortnightly to the Magistrate .

(6) Progress Report to Informant/Victim — Section 193 BNSS
The police report must include details of the sequence of custody in case of electronic devices. The officer must inform the informant or victim of investigation progress within 90 days. After filing of charge sheet, further investigation must be completed within 90 days, with extension only with Court permission .

5. ARREST, BAIL, AND INVESTIGATION
Cognisable and Non-Cognisable Offences
Cognisable offences — serious offences allowing police to arrest without a court warrant .

Non-cognisable offences — the police officer requires a court's permission before making an arrest .

Bailable and Non-Bailable Offences
Bailable offences — the arrested person may seek bail from the police officer or court as a matter of right, upon presenting a bond/undertaking .

Non-bailable offences — bail cannot be claimed as a right and must be applied for before the jurisdictional court .

Arrest Without Warrant — Section 55 BNSS
When a police station in-charge or investigating officer deputes a subordinate to arrest without a warrant:

Must deliver an order in writing specifying the person to be arrested and the offence

Before making the arrest, the subordinate officer must notify the person of the substance of the order

If required, must show the person the order 

Seizure of Property — Section 106 BNSS
Replaces Section 102 CrPC, governing the power of police to seize property during criminal investigations, including bank account freezing in cybercrime cases, with reporting obligations to Magistrates .

Burden of Proof and Limitation
Burden of proof — rests with the prosecution, which must prove guilt "beyond reasonable doubt," unless a statute specifically provides for reverse onus (e.g., PMLA) .

Limitation periods under BNSS :

Offences punishable only with fine: 6 months

Punishable with imprisonment up to 1 year: 1 year

Punishable with imprisonment 1–3 years: 3 years

Punishable with imprisonment >3 years: No limitation

Extraterritorial Reach
Sections 3 and 4 of BNS, along with Section 208 of BNSS, recognise extraterritorial application of Indian criminal law. Indian citizens can be punished for offences committed outside India. A person outside India can also be punished for an offence in India under the BNS. Section 48 of BNS further makes it an offence to abet a crime in India from outside the country .

6. PRELIMINARY ENQUIRY UNDER BNSS
Position Under CrPC
The concept of preliminary enquiry was not codified under CrPC but was read into it by judicial pronouncements. The Supreme Court in Lalita Kumari v. Government of Uttar Pradesh (2014) 2 SCC 1 held that FIR registration is mandatory if information discloses a cognisable offence. Preliminary enquiry, if conducted, was only to determine whether a cognisable offence was disclosed — not to verify credibility .

Position Under BNSS — Significant Departure
Section 173(3) BNSS — Discretion Introduced
For cognisable offences punishable with imprisonment of three years or more but less than seven years, the police may, with prior approval of an officer not below the rank of Deputy Superintendent of Police (DSP), either:

Conduct a preliminary enquiry within 14 days to ascertain whether a "prima facie" case exists, or

Directly proceed with investigation if such a prima facie case exists 

Supreme Court Ruling — Imran Pratapgadhi v. State of Gujarat (2025) :
Even if information discloses a cognisable offence, the police are not obligated to file an FIR and have discretion to conduct a preliminary enquiry to determine if a prima facie case exists. If the police conclude a prima facie case exists, they must file the FIR; if not, they inform the informant, who may pursue other remedies .

Conceptual Challenges:

What constitutes a "prima facie case" is not defined in the BNSS

If information already discloses a cognisable offence, what additional threshold must be met to conclude a prima facie case exists?

The credibility or merits of information is not a precondition for FIR registration — this was settled in Lalita Kumari 

Impact on Informant's Rights:
Under Section 175(3) BNSS, the Magistrate is now statutorily mandated to consider the police's submissions explaining their refusal to register the FIR before issuing any direction to investigate (Supreme Court in Om Prakash Ambadkar v. State of Maharashtra, 2025 SCC Online SC 238) .

7. CYBERCRIME POLICING
E-Zero FIR — Pilot for Cybercrime (May 2025)
The Home Ministry launched the e-Zero FIR pilot in Delhi to automatically convert complaints of financial cybercrime exceeding ₹10 lakh lodged through the National Cybercrime Reporting Portal or the 1930 helpline into a formal FIR .

Features:

Combines Delhi Police's e-FIR platform with NCRB's CCTNS infrastructure

Cuts out discretionary delays in FIR registration

Victims can monitor the crime through a documented, accountable investigative process

Reduces evidentiary preservation time 

Zero-FIR Mechanism:

Allows an FIR for a cognisable offence to be registered at any police station, regardless of where the crime was committed

Report assigned the serial number "0" and subsequently transferred to the competent police station

Traces to Section 173 BNSS, bolstered by Section 175(2) BNSS which bars challenge to police investigation for want of territorial jurisdiction 

Limitations:

The ₹10 lakh threshold leaves many mid-value victims outside its ambit

Requires clear SOPs and consistent oversight to filter frivolous or malicious complaints 

Jurisdiction — IT Act Cases
Orissa High Court (August 2025) — Jayanta Kumar Das v. State of Odisha:
Concurrent jurisdiction applies to IT Act offences. The Court clarified that Sections 78 and 80 of the IT Act do not divest general police stations of competence to investigate; they condition such competence by prescribing the minimum rank of the Investigating Officer (not below Inspector). A notification granting "exclusive jurisdiction" to CID Cyber Crime Police Station must be read alongside "concurrent jurisdiction all over the State" preserved in the same notification .

Section 78 IT Act — Rank Requirement
Any police officer not below the rank of Inspector is competent to investigate offences under the IT Act .

8. CROSS-BORDER AND INTERNATIONAL COOPERATION
Mutual Legal Assistance Treaties (MLAT)
India has MLATs with 14 countries for mutual legal assistance in criminal matters. The Ministry of Home Affairs is the nodal ministry and central authority for all such assistance. For countries not covered under an MLAT, requests are made based on reciprocity through the concerned mission/embassy .

Extradition
India has extradition treaties with 48 foreign states (including USA, UK, Switzerland) and extradition arrangements with 12 foreign states (including Sweden and Singapore). The Indian Extradition Act, 1962 governs the process .

INTERPOL
India has been an INTERPOL member since 1949

Red Notices issued at India's request: 100 in 2023, 107 in 2024, 56 in first half of 2025

India joined 52 countries in INTERPOL's Silver Notice pilot phase (January 2025) — a tool to recover assets and request asset-related information across borders

First Purple Notice issued by ED on 21 August 2025, notifying 196 member states of a new trade-based money laundering modus operandi 

FATF
India has been an FATF member since 2010

FATF has commended India's efforts against illicit finance, placing it in the "regular follow-up" category — the highest rating 

9. FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS
Q1: What is the difference between cognisable and non-cognisable offences?
A: Cognisable offences are serious offences where police can arrest without a court warrant. Non-cognisable offences require a court's permission before arrest .

Q2: How has police custody period changed under the new laws?
A: The maximum period for police custody has increased from 15 days under CrPC to 60 or 90 days under BNSS, depending on the nature of the offence .

Q3: What is a Zero-FIR?
A: An FIR registered at any police station regardless of jurisdiction. It is assigned serial number "0" and subsequently transferred to the competent police station .

Q4: Can a police station refuse to register an FIR for lack of territorial jurisdiction?
A: No. Section 175(2) BNSS bars challenge to police investigation for want of territorial jurisdiction. The Zero-FIR mechanism ensures registration regardless of jurisdiction .

Q5: What is the procedure for registering a cybercrime complaint?
A: Complaints can be lodged through the National Cybercrime Reporting Portal or the 1930 helpline. For financial cybercrime exceeding ₹10 lakh, the e-Zero FIR pilot automatically converts the complaint into a formal FIR .

Q6: When is preliminary enquiry required under BNSS?
A: For cognisable offences punishable with imprisonment of three years or more but less than seven years, the police may, with DSP approval, conduct a preliminary enquiry within 14 days to ascertain whether a prima facie case exists .

Q7: What are the limitation periods for prosecuting offences under BNSS?
A: Six months for offences punishable with fine only; one year for imprisonment up to one year; three years for imprisonment between one and three years; no limitation for imprisonment exceeding three years .
