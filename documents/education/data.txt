EMPLOYMENT & LABOUR LAW — INDIA (Legal Knowledge Base for RAG)
1. CONSTITUTIONAL & STATUTORY FRAMEWORK
Constitutional Basis
Article 14 (Equality before law): Prohibits discrimination in employment on grounds of sex, religion, etc. 

Article 15 (Prohibition of discrimination): Empowers the state to make special provisions for women, children, and socially/educationally backward classes. The Supreme Court has reaffirmed that dismissal of a female employee based on marital status constitutes unconstitutional gender discrimination .

Article 16 (Equality of opportunity in public employment): Guarantees equal opportunity for all citizens in matters relating to employment or appointment to any office under the State.

Article 21 (Right to life & personal liberty): In the employment context, this encompasses the right to a safe working environment, fair treatment, and protection from sexual harassment.

The Four Labour Codes (Key Reform)
The Indian government has introduced four Labour Codes to consolidate 29 existing labour laws. While not fully in force, significant progress on implementation is expected in 2025–26 .

Code on Wages, 2019: Consolidates laws relating to wages, bonuses, and incidental matters .

Code on Social Security, 2020: Consolidates laws relating to social security for workers .

Industrial Relations Code, 2020: Consolidates laws relating to trade unions, conditions of employment, and settlement of industrial disputes .

Occupational Safety, Health, and Working Conditions Code, 2020: Consolidates laws relating to occupational safety and health of persons employed in an establishment .

Key Supporting Legislations
Industrial Disputes Act, 1947: Governs termination ('retrenchment') of 'workmen' category employees, requiring notice, compensation, and adherence to the 'last-in-first-out' principle .

Sexual Harassment of Women at Workplace Act, 2013: The definition of 'workplace' includes a 'house', covering remote working arrangements .

Maternity Benefit Act, 1961: Entitles women with at least 80 days of service in the preceding 12 months to 26 weeks of paid maternity leave .

Factories Act & State Shops & Establishments Acts: Lay down minimum standards for working hours, leaves, and overtime wages. The statutory working hours are typically a maximum of nine hours each day and 48 hours per week .

2. KEY DEFINITIONS & CATEGORISATIONS (for retrieval precision)
Workmen v. Non-Workmen: A crucial distinction under labour law.

Workmen: Individuals employed to do manual, clerical, skilled, unskilled, technical, or supervisory work, drawing wages of ₹10,000 or less, and not exercising managerial functions. They are entitled to greater statutory protections (e.g., under the ID Act) .

Non-Workmen: Typically white-collar, managerial, or administrative employees. Their terms are primarily governed by their employment contracts, company policies, and state Shops & Establishment Acts .

Retrenchment: The termination of a workman's service for any reason whatsoever, otherwise than as a punishment inflicted by way of disciplinary action .

Organised Crime: Under the BNS, 2023, this is defined to include 'cybercrime' and economic offences committed by a group acting in concert for material or financial benefit .

Electronic Communication: Defined under the BNSS and used in BNS, it means communication of written, verbal, pictorial, or video content transmitted through an electronic device or in electronic form .

3. EMPLOYMENT TERMS & CONDITIONS
Written Contracts & Appointment Letters
While not universally mandated, most S&E Acts require issuing an appointment letter. Under the OSH Code, employers will be required to issue an appointment letter to every employee specifying minimum prescribed information .

Certain terms, such as confidentiality and non-disclosure, may be implied by custom and industry practice, but it is recommended to encapsulate all terms in a written contract .

Working Hours & Overtime
Statutory working hours are typically 9 hours per day and 48 hours per week. Overtime wages, paid at twice the ordinary rate, are required for work beyond these limits .

Employment of women during night hours is restricted in most states but is permitted in several states (e.g., Karnataka, Maharashtra, Delhi) subject to compliance with prescribed safety and transport conditions .

Leaves & Holidays
Employees are entitled to paid annual leave (typically 240 days of work to determine eligibility, to be reduced to 180 days under the OSH Code), sick leave, casual leave, and national holidays .

Unavailed annual leaves can be carried forward, and under the OSH Code, employees will be entitled to encashment of leave at the end of the calendar year .

Social Security & Benefits
Provident Fund: For establishments covered under the EPF Act, employers contribute 12% and employees contribute 12% of monthly pay. This also applies to 'international workers' (foreign nationals), subject to social security agreements (SSAs) with their home country .

Maternity Leave: 26 weeks of paid leave for women who have worked for at least 80 days in the preceding 12 months. The Ministry of Labour encourages work-from-home options for nursing mothers .

Paternity Leave: Not statutorily recognised in India, though industry practice, especially in the IT/ITES sector, often grants 5–7 days .

4. TERMINATION, REDUNDANCY & BUSINESS TRANSFERS
Termination of Workmen (Retrenchment)
The ID Act mandates a one-month written notice (in some cases three months) and payment of retrenchment compensation. The principle of 'last-in, first-out' must be adhered to .

Employers must provide a valid and bona fide reason for termination. Termination as a 'colourable exercise' of rights or by way of victimisation is considered an unfair labour practice, and employees can challenge it and seek reinstatement and back wages .

A domestic enquiry is required before termination for misconduct .

Termination of Non-Workmen
Governed by the employment contract and state-specific S&E Acts. Typically, one month's notice or payment in lieu of notice is required .

Business Transfers
In a business transfer, workmen with at least one year of continuous service are entitled to notice and retrenchment compensation unless their service is uninterrupted and terms are not worsened post-transfer .

The impact on non-workmen depends on their employment contract terms .

Restrictive Covenants (Non-Compete)
Under the Contract Act, any agreement in restraint of trade is void. A non-compete clause is generally enforceable only during the term of employment. Post-termination non-compete clauses are considered void and unenforceable by the Supreme Court .

5. DIGITALISATION & CYBER LAW OVERLAP
New Criminal Laws & Cybercrime
The Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita (BNS), 2023, which replaced the IPC, now explicitly includes 'cybercrime' under the definition of 'organised crime' .

Electronic Communications as a Means for Offences: BNS recognises 'electronic communication' as a medium for committing offences such as hate speech, sedition (reframed as "acts endangering sovereignty"), and defamation .

Expanded Scope of Abetment: A person can now be held liable for abetting an offence from a place outside India, potentially holding online platforms operating outside India liable for offences on their platforms .

Electronic Evidence in Labour Disputes
Under the Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam (BSA), 2023, electronic records (emails, messages, digital documents) are treated as primary evidence .

For admissibility, electronic evidence must meet specific authenticity criteria, and a certificate signed by the person in charge is required to confirm how the record was produced and that the device functioned properly .

Trials and proceedings may be conducted through electronic mode, and Courts can direct the production of electronic communication devices for investigation .

6. FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS (RAG-friendly Q&A pairs)
Q1: What is the difference between a 'workman' and a 'non-workman' employee?
A: A 'workman' is an employee who does manual, clerical, skilled, unskilled, technical, or supervisory work, draws wages of ₹10,000 or less, and is not in a managerial role. 'Non-workmen' are typically white-collar, managerial, or administrative employees . Workmen are entitled to greater statutory protections, particularly under the Industrial Disputes Act, 1947, while non-workmen's terms are primarily governed by their contract .

Q2: What is the procedure for terminating a 'workman' employee?
A: The process, known as 'retrenchment', generally requires a one-month written notice or payment of wages in lieu of notice, payment of retrenchment compensation, and adherence to the 'last-in, first-out' principle . The employer must have a bona fide business reason, and any termination by way of victimisation is considered an unfair labour practice .

Q3: Can a company enforce a non-compete clause in India?
A: Under the Contract Act, any agreement in restraint of trade is void. The Supreme Court has held that post-termination non-compete clauses are void and unenforceable . However, non-compete clauses during the term of employment are generally enforceable .

Q4: What are the statutory working hours and overtime provisions?
A: Indian labour laws typically prescribe a maximum of 9 hours of work per day and 48 hours per week . Employees who work in excess of these hours are entitled to overtime wages, typically at twice the ordinary rate .

Q5: How has the new criminal law (BNS) impacted cybercrime in the employment context?
A: The BNS has introduced significant changes by recognising 'electronic communication' as a medium for committing offences like extortion or hate speech. Crucially, it now categorises 'cybercrime' under the umbrella of 'organised crime', bringing it within a more rigorous punishment framework .

Q6: Is an email or a screenshot of a WhatsApp message admissible as evidence in a labour dispute?
A: Yes, under the Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam (BSA), 2023, electronic records are now treated as primary evidence, which is a major reform . However, they must meet authenticity criteria and be accompanied by a certificate from the person in charge to be admissible .
