# CONSUMER LAW — INDIA (Legal Knowledge Base for RAG)

## 1. CONSTITUTIONAL BASIS

### Article 19(1)(g) — Right to trade, subject to reasonable restriction
Businesses derive their right to sell goods/services from this article, but the state can impose reasonable restrictions in the interest of consumer welfare — labelling requirements, safety standards, and licensing conditions under sector laws (Food Safety and Standards Act, Legal Metrology Act, Drugs and Cosmetics Act) are treated as valid restrictions under Article 19(6).

### Article 21 — Right to life and personal liberty
Courts have read the right to a safe and healthy environment, and the right against adulterated food/unsafe products, into Article 21. Medical negligence and food-safety litigation frequently invokes Article 21 alongside statutory consumer remedies.

### Article 14 — Equality before law
Used to challenge arbitrary classification — for instance, differential pecuniary-jurisdiction slabs for consumer commissions, or discriminatory refusal of service to a class of consumers. The Supreme Court has upheld Parliament's competence to fix pecuniary jurisdiction slabs based on the value of consideration paid, holding this satisfies the Article 14 test of intelligible differentia and rational nexus.

### Article 32 and Article 226 — Constitutional remedies
Writ petitions before the Supreme Court and High Courts are used to challenge CCPA orders alleged to be arbitrary, blanket bans on products without due process, or denial of essential services (electricity, water, insurance) amounting to a violation of Article 14/21.

### Legislative competence
Consumer protection falls under Entry 7 of the Concurrent List ("Contracts") and various Union List entries touching trade and commerce (Entry 42, List I read with residuary powers), allowing both Parliament and, historically, State legislatures to enact protective measures — though the Consumer Protection Act, 2019 is a central enactment applicable across India.

## 2. KEY STATUTES GOVERNING CONSUMER PROTECTION IN INDIA

### 2.1 Consumer Protection Act, 2019 (Act No. 35 of 2019)
Replaced the Consumer Protection Act, 1986, and came into force on 20 July 2020. It was enacted to address modern challenges such as e-commerce fraud, misleading advertisements, and product liability, alongside strengthening the older forum-based redressal system.

Key definitions and provisions:
- **Section 2(7)** defines "consumer" as a person who buys goods or hires/avails services for consideration, but excludes a person who obtains goods/services for resale or commercial purpose (except when used exclusively for self-employment/livelihood).
- **Section 2(11)** defines "deficiency" broadly, including any fault, imperfection, shortcoming, or inadequacy in the quality, nature, or manner of performance of a service — this definition has since been extended by consumer commissions to cover data-security lapses (a data leak has been treated as a "deficiency of service").
- **Section 2(16)/2(1)(o)** defines "e-commerce" as buying or selling of goods or services, including digital products, over a digital or electronic network — covering marketplace, inventory, and social-commerce models.
- **Section 2(35)** defines a "product liability action" as a complaint before a District, State, or National Commission for compensation for harm caused by a defective product.
- **Section 10** establishes the Central Consumer Protection Authority (CCPA) to promote, protect, and enforce consumer rights as a class, with powers to investigate unfair trade practices, recall unsafe goods, order reimbursement, and impose penalties on manufacturers and endorsers of misleading advertisements.
- **Section 21** empowers the CCPA to impose a penalty of up to ₹10 lakh on a manufacturer or endorser for a first misleading advertisement, rising to ₹50 lakh for subsequent offences, and permits an endorser to be prohibited from endorsing that product/service for up to 1 year (3 years for repeat offences).
- **Chapter VI, Sections 82–87 — Product Liability**: For the first time in Indian legislative history, an entire chapter is dedicated to product liability, separate from the Sale of Goods Act, 1930 and the Indian Contract Act, 1872. A product manufacturer, product service provider, or product seller can be held strictly liable where a defective product or deficient service causes "harm" — defined to include personal injury, illness, death, property damage (other than to the product itself), and mental agony, but excluding pure commercial/economic loss. A product seller who is not the manufacturer is liable only in specific circumstances — e.g., where it exercised substantial control over design/manufacture, altered/modified the product, or made an independent express warranty that failed.
- **Section 88** — makes an endorser/celebrity liable if an advertisement is misleading, subject to a due-diligence defence.
- **Section 89** — criminalises false or misleading advertisements prejudicial to consumer interest, punishable with imprisonment up to 2 years and fine, escalating for repeat offences.
- **Sections 49(2) and 59(2)** — empower the State and National Commissions to declare unfair contract terms in consumer contracts void.

### 2.2 Three-tier Consumer Redressal Commissions and pecuniary jurisdiction
The Act establishes District Commissions, State Commissions, and a National Commission. Under the original 2019 Act, jurisdiction was fixed at up to ₹1 crore (District), ₹1–10 crore (State), and above ₹10 crore (National), based on the value of goods/services paid as consideration (replacing the 1986 Act's method of also counting the compensation claimed, which had encouraged forum shopping). This was subsequently revised by the **Consumer Protection (Jurisdiction of the District Commission, the State Commission and the National Commission) Rules, 2021**, which lowered the slabs to reduce pendency at the National Commission: District Commissions now hear claims up to ₹50 lakh, State Commissions ₹50 lakh–₹2 crore, and the National Commission above ₹2 crore.
- Territorial jurisdiction was liberalised in 2019 to allow a complainant to file where they reside or personally work for gain, in addition to where the opposite party resides/carries on business — removing the earlier hurdle for consumers litigating against businesses with no local branch.
- Complaints can be filed electronically through the e-Daakhil/e-Jagriti portals, and commissions are required to resolve disputes within specified timeframes (typically 3–5 months, extendable where testing of the goods is required).
- Appeals lie from District to State Commission, State to National Commission, and from the National Commission to the Supreme Court; an appeal involving a monetary award generally requires a pre-deposit (50% of the amount ordered, subject to specific provisions) before it is entertained.

### 2.3 Consumer Protection (E-Commerce) Rules, 2020
Notified in July 2020 under the 2019 Act to regulate online marketplaces and inventory-model sellers. Key obligations:
- E-commerce entities must be incorporated as a company under the Companies Act, 2013, or be a registered foreign entity/branch.
- **Affirmative consent** — consent for any purchase must be recorded through explicit affirmative action; pre-ticked checkboxes and automatically bundled "add-on" products are prohibited.
- Entities must display their legal name, headquarters, and contact/grievance-officer details, and disclose seller information (name, address, contact) for traceability.
- A grievance redressal mechanism with a nodal officer must be maintained, with complaints resolved within 30 days (commonly referenced as a 1-month window).
- Pricing algorithms, sponsored ranking criteria, and any differential treatment/discrimination between consumers of the same class must be disclosed and are otherwise prohibited.
- Sellers must independently disclose unit price, taxes, delivery charges, and comply with the Legal Metrology (Packaged Commodities) Rules; sellers remain responsible for warranty obligations of goods/services sold.
- No person convicted of an offence carrying punishment of 5 years or more may be part of the promoter/management team of an e-commerce entity.

### 2.4 Bureau of Indian Standards Act, 2016
Provides for compulsory certification/hallmarking (e.g., BIS hallmark on gold jewellery, ISI mark on specified products) and empowers recall of non-conforming goods — frequently invoked alongside CCPA product-safety actions.

### 2.5 Food Safety and Standards Act, 2006 (FSSA)
Governs food adulteration and mislabelling; the Food Safety and Standards Authority of India (FSSAI) can order product recalls and prosecute for unsafe food, running in parallel with consumer-forum remedies for the same conduct.

### 2.6 Legal Metrology Act, 2009
Regulates weights, measures, and mandatory declarations (MRP, net quantity, manufacturing/expiry date) on packaged commodities; violations are a common basis for "unfair trade practice" complaints before consumer commissions.

### 2.7 Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam, 2023 (BSA) — evidentiary relevance
Electronic records such as screenshots, digital receipts, and app transaction logs are admissible in consumer proceedings subject to authenticity/certification requirements under the BSA (which replaced the Indian Evidence Act, 1872, from 1 July 2024).

## 3. REGULATORY AND GRIEVANCE MECHANISMS

### 3.1 Central Consumer Protection Authority (CCPA)
A regulator distinct from the consumer commissions, empowered under Section 10 to act suo motu or on a complaint to investigate unfair trade practices/violations of consumer rights as a class, order recalls, direct discontinuation of misleading ads, and impose penalties — a class-action-style enforcement tool that did not exist under the 1986 Act.

### 3.2 National Consumer Helpline (NCH) / INGRAM
A pre-litigation grievance-redressal platform (1915 helpline, integrated with the e-Daakhil portal) where consumers can register complaints against companies before escalating to a formal commission filing; many companies are onboarded as "convergence partners" who respond directly through the platform.

### 3.3 Consumer Mediation Cells
Attached to each District, State, and National Commission under the 2019 Act, enabling court-annexed mediation as an alternative to adjudication, particularly for disputes where an amicable settlement is feasible.

### 3.4 Sector regulators with overlapping jurisdiction
Insurance (IRDAI), banking (RBI/Banking Ombudsman), telecom (TRAI), and real estate (RERA authorities) each have their own grievance mechanisms; a consumer often has a choice of forum, and pursuing a sectoral remedy does not automatically bar a consumer-commission complaint for the same deficiency, though courts examine whether the special law impliedly excludes CPA jurisdiction (as considered in real-estate disputes overlapping with RERA).

## 4. LANDMARK CASE LAW

### 4.1 Indian Medical Association v. V.P. Shantha & Ors., (1995) 6 SCC 651
- <cite index="22-1">The Supreme Court held that medical services rendered for a fee fall within the definition of "service" under the Consumer Protection Act, making doctors and hospitals answerable before consumer forums.</cite>
- <cite index="22-1">Applying a Bolam-type standard, the Court held that practitioners must exercise a reasonable standard of care, skill, and judgment, and failure to do so can found a negligence claim under the Act.</cite>
- This remains the foundational judgment bringing medical negligence within consumer law rather than leaving patients to civil-court litigation alone; in November 2024 the Supreme Court declined to refer the ruling to a larger bench, confirming that medical professionals continue to be covered.

### 4.2 Lucknow Development Authority v. M.K. Gupta, (1994) 1 SCC 243
- <cite index="21-1">A homebuyer complained against the Lucknow Development Authority for delay in handing over possession of a flat, and the Supreme Court held the statutory authority liable for deficiency in service.</cite>
- <cite index="25-1">The judgment established that government bodies, municipal authorities, housing boards, and public-sector service providers qualify as "service providers" under consumer law and cannot escape accountability by invoking their governmental character.</cite>

### 4.3 Spring Meadows Hospital v. Harjol Ahluwalia, (1998) 4 SCC 39
Extended consumer-forum standing to parents/family members of a patient who suffered due to hospital negligence, recognising that the person who pays for treatment (the parent) and the person who receives it (the child) can both be treated as affected consumers for compensation purposes.

### 4.4 Bar of Indian Lawyers v. D.K. Gandhi (2024)
The Supreme Court held that legal services rendered by advocates do not fall within the definition of "service" under the CPA, distinguishing the legal profession from medicine on the basis that advocates function as officers of the court without a comparable objective standard of care, and that professional misconduct by advocates is separately regulated under the Advocates Act — narrowing V.P. Shantha's reach rather than overruling it.

### 4.5 HDFC Bank v. Balwinder Singh (NCDRC)
The National Commission held banks/NBFCs liable in deficiency-of-service actions where recovery agents used coercive or unauthorised repossession tactics (e.g., forcible seizure of a financed vehicle without following due process), reinforcing that recovery must follow the RBI Fair Practices Code even outside a strictly banking-law forum.

### 4.6 Ghaziabad Development Authority v. Balbir Singh & U.T. Chandigarh Administration v. Amarjeet Singh
A line of Supreme Court rulings on interest/compensation payable by development authorities for delayed possession or defective allotment of housing plots, reinforcing that statutory development authorities are answerable in the same manner as private developers for deficiency in service.

## 5. COMMON DISPUTE CATEGORIES (for RAG retrieval tagging)

1. Defective goods and product liability claims (Chapter VI, Sections 82–87).
2. Deficiency of service — delayed possession of flats/plots, banking services, insurance claim repudiation, courier/logistics failures.
3. Medical negligence claims against doctors/hospitals (post V.P. Shantha), including consent and standard-of-care disputes.
4. E-commerce disputes — non-delivery, counterfeit goods, refund/return denial, dark patterns, and pre-ticked consent violations under the 2020 E-Commerce Rules.
5. Misleading/false advertisements and celebrity endorsement liability (Sections 88–89).
6. Unfair trade practices — false representation of quality/standard, bait advertising, hoarding, black-marketing.
7. Unfair contract terms in standard-form consumer contracts (Sections 49(2)/59(2)).
8. Data breaches/data-security lapses treated as deficiency of service.
9. Real estate disputes overlapping with RERA — jurisdictional choice between RERA authority and consumer commission.
10. Cross-border e-commerce disputes involving foreign sellers who disregard Indian jurisdiction/summons.
11. Dynamic/algorithmic pricing and undisclosed ranking criteria on digital platforms.
12. Product recalls and food safety violations (overlap with FSSAI).
13. Weights, measures, and MRP-related unfair trade practice complaints (Legal Metrology Act overlap).
14. Insurance mis-selling and claim repudiation (overlap with IRDAI grievance mechanisms).
15. Class-action style CCPA proceedings against manufacturers/e-commerce entities for systemic violations.

## 7. KEY DEFINITIONS / GLOSSARY (for retrieval precision)

- **Consumer:** A person who buys goods or avails services for consideration, excluding those who do so for resale or commercial purpose (except self-employment/livelihood use) — Section 2(7).
- **Deficiency:** Any fault, imperfection, shortcoming, or inadequacy in the quality, nature, or manner of performance of a service — Section 2(11).
- **Unfair Trade Practice:** A trade practice adopting unfair methods/deceptive practices for promoting sale/use of goods or services, including false representations, bait advertising, and non-compliance with mandated standards.
- **Product Liability Action:** A complaint before a consumer commission seeking compensation for harm caused by a defective product — Section 2(35).
- **Harm (for product liability):** Personal injury, illness, death, property damage (excluding the product itself), and mental agony/emotional distress; excludes pure commercial/economic loss.
- **Product Seller vs Product Manufacturer:** A manufacturer designs/produces the product; a seller (including an e-commerce marketplace acting as an "electronic service provider") is liable only in defined circumstances such as exercising substantial control, modifying the product, or breaching an independent warranty.
- **Unfair Contract:** A contract between a manufacturer/trader/service provider and a consumer having terms that cause significant change in the rights of the consumer, which a State or National Commission may declare void.
- **e-Daakhil / e-Jagriti:** Government portals enabling electronic filing of consumer complaints and virtual hearings before the commissions.
- **CCPA:** Central Consumer Protection Authority — a regulator (distinct from the adjudicatory commissions) with powers to act suo motu against unfair trade practices affecting consumers as a class.
- **Affirmative Consent:** Consent obtained through a clear, opt-in action by the consumer, as opposed to pre-ticked or default-selected options — mandated under the E-Commerce Rules, 2020.

## 8. RECENT REGULATORY TRENDS (context for evolving queries)

- The 2021 revision of pecuniary jurisdiction slabs (lowering the District/State/National thresholds) was a direct response to overload at the National Commission after the 2019 Act's higher slabs shifted a large volume of mid-value cases upward, causing delay defeating the Act's speedy-redressal objective.
- Courts and commissions are increasingly treating data breaches and cybersecurity lapses by service providers as an actionable "deficiency of service," extending consumer law into data-protection territory even though a dedicated data protection statute (Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023) now also applies.
- Cross-border e-commerce enforcement remains weak in practice — the E-Commerce Rules require disputes to be resolved in the consumer's home jurisdiction, but enforcement against foreign sellers who ignore Indian summons depends on mutual legal assistance and voluntary cooperation, which are inconsistent.
- Algorithmic/dynamic pricing and personalised-pricing complaints are a growing category; while no final appellate ruling has settled the issue, such practices are argued to fall within the Act's general prohibition on unfair trade practices.
- The Bar of Indian Lawyers v. D.K. Gandhi (2024) ruling narrowed V.P. Shantha's scope by excluding legal services from "service" under the Act, and is being watched for its effect on other regulated professions (chartered accountants, architects) that may seek similar carve-outs.
- The CCPA has increasingly used its class-action-style powers against e-commerce entities and manufacturers for systemic dark-pattern and misleading-advertisement violations, rather than leaving every complaint to individual commission litigation.

## 9. FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS (RAG-friendly Q&A pairs)

**Q1: I bought a defective appliance — where do I file a complaint and for how much?**
A: File before the District Commission if the value of goods/services paid as consideration is up to ₹50 lakh (current 2021 threshold), the State Commission if it is between ₹50 lakh and ₹2 crore, and the National Commission above that. You may file where you reside or where the opposite party resides/carries on business.

**Q2: Can I sue a hospital in a consumer forum for medical negligence?**
A: Yes — under Indian Medical Association v. V.P. Shantha, paid medical services fall within "service" under the Act, so patients (and, per Spring Meadows Hospital, their family members) can seek compensation before a consumer commission rather than only through a civil suit.

**Q3: An e-commerce site added an extra item to my cart with a pre-checked box — is that legal?**
A: No — the Consumer Protection (E-Commerce) Rules, 2020 require affirmative, opt-in consent for every item purchased; pre-ticked checkboxes and automatically bundled add-ons are not valid consent and can be challenged as an unfair trade practice.

**Q4: A celebrity endorsed a product that turned out to be fraudulent — can they be held liable?**
A: Yes — Section 88 makes an endorser liable if the advertisement is misleading, subject to a due-diligence defence, and the CCPA can additionally bar the endorser from future endorsements for up to 1 year (3 years for repeat violations) under Section 21.

**Q5: My complaint was rejected/undercompensated by the District Commission — what next?**
A: You may appeal to the State Commission, and from there to the National Commission, and finally to the Supreme Court; appeals against a monetary award generally require a pre-deposit (commonly 50% of the awarded sum) before being entertained.

**Q6: Can a government development authority be sued for delay in giving me possession of my flat?**
A: Yes — per Lucknow Development Authority v. M.K. Gupta, statutory authorities are "service providers" under the Act and are liable for deficiency in service just like private developers, though you should also check whether RERA offers an overlapping or more direct remedy for the same delay.

**Q7: Does filing a complaint with the CCPA stop me from also filing before a consumer commission?**
A: No — the CCPA's class-action-style enforcement (recalls, penalties, discontinuation of misleading ads) is separate from an individual's right to seek personal compensation before a District/State/National Commission for the same deficiency; the two can proceed independently.

---

- Always confirm the current pecuniary jurisdiction slab in force (the 2021 Rules revised the original 2019 Act figures downward), since forum choice and appeal strategy depend on it.
- For product liability claims, check whether the defendant is the manufacturer, the service provider, or merely a seller/marketplace, since sellers who are not manufacturers are liable only in specific circumstances under Sections 84–86.
- For e-commerce complaints, verify whether the entity is a marketplace model (intermediary, generally not liable for the underlying product defect) or an inventory model (direct seller, bearing full liability).
- For medical negligence queries, note the 2024 Bar of Indian Lawyers ruling narrowed (but did not overturn) V.P. Shantha, and confirm the professional category involved before assuming consumer-forum jurisdiction automatically applies.
- Where a dispute could also be pursued under a sectoral regulator (RERA, IRDAI, RBI Ombudsman, TRAI), flag the overlapping remedy and note that courts examine whether the special statute impliedly excludes CPA jurisdiction on the facts.
- Electronic evidence (screenshots, app logs, digital receipts) is admissible under the BSA, 2023, but authenticity/certification requirements should be checked before relying on it as sole proof.
